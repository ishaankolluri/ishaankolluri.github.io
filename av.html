Humanizing autonomous vehicles
I started a research project with two of my classmates in August 2017. We identified an opportunity to interact with autonomous vehicles in Pittsburgh, and through research, identified a cognitive dissonance between how pedestrians thought and behaved around them. To that end, we designed a digital windshield interface to quickly communicate how autonomous vehicles perceive and interpret their surroundings.

whitepaper

Our designs borrow from street signage to communicate intent as quickly as possible.

Sometimes autonomous vehicles are scary.
Through qualitative interviews and paper prototyping, we found that while many people were vocal about their support of autonomous vehicles in the future, some were cautious or uncomfortable when confronted with self-driving cars on the road. Our findings primarily attributed this to a lack of outward communication from the car to nearby pedestrians. This was exceptionally important in ambiguous on-road situations, such as crosswalks.

Designing for the transition infrastructure
It became clear to us early on that we weren't designing for an idealistic future where pedestrians would only interact with machine drivers. The hypothesis—for the next two to three decades, the roads will have a substantial mix of human and autonomous drivers.

Thinking fast, not slow.
Drawing inspiration from literature, such as Daniel Kahneman's Thinking Fast and Slow, we identified a rough metric for success: users should be able to process our display in a near-instantaneous fashion. Pedestrians interact with human drivers through eye contact and gestures, resulting in speedy decision-making at crosswalks. We wanted our design to be better than existing solutions from companies like Nissan or BMW.

Testing simple concepts with locals in exchange for cookies.

Long live skeuomorphism.
After user testing different ways to represent the relationship between the human and the car, we decided to stick with what we were familiar with. In order to keep the interaction between our display and the pedestrian fleeting and effortless, we decided to roughly borrow from physical concepts people are familiar with: signs from pedestrian traffic lights. We decided to represent the human on the windshield, with various states to communicate whether the pedestrian is safe to cross—which it normally always is.

The vehicle state is almost always green—pedestrians have the right of way.

A basic workflow of the autonomous vehicle display in different on-road scenarios.

Optimization and accessibility
We purposefully avoiding overdesigning this interface. There are a variety of weather and light conditions that this display needs to account for, and given the time, we would like to test a physical prototype that can work in multiple usage contexts.

We struggled to identify situations where an autonomous vehicle would need to assert its right of way—there are very view legal circumstances for this. People told us they didn't like the confrontational nature of an alert, and in the future, we'd like to continue heavily iterating on the use cases of a "red" state for pedestrians.

Humans also process sound faster than sight. Designing with sound in mind would make this experience friendly to those with visual disabilities, and it's an important next step that has to be discussed as we enter a world with both human and machine drivers.
